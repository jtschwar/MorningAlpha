## Part 1 — LGBM Score Anti-Correlation Diagnosis

### Step 1A: Training Target Inspection

**How `forward_63d_rank` is computed** (`dataset.py:1655–1658`):
```python
df[f"{col}_rank"] = df.groupby("date")[col].transform(_rank_norm_series)
```
Where `_rank_norm_series` cross-sectionally rank-normalizes to (−1, 1) using average rank:
```python
ranks = x.rank(method="average")
return (2.0 * (ranks - 1.0) / (n - 1.0) - 1.0)
```

**Label type:** Absolute raw return — NOT market-excess, NOT sector-relative.
The `forward_63d_market_excess_rank` and `forward_63d_sector_relative_rank` columns exist
but are NOT used as the primary training target for `lgbm_breakout_v10`.

**Label availability by month:**

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oct 2025 | 96,109 | 96,109 | 0 | ≈0.000 |
| Nov 2025 | 84,129 | 84,129 | 0 | ≈0.000 |
| Dec 2025 | 101,569 | **76,819** | 24,750 | **−0.033** |

**Critical finding:** Dec 2025 labels are fully resolved. Their 63d forward window
lands in **Feb–Mar 2026** — the peak of the tariff selloff. The mean rank of −0.033
(slightly negative) understates the issue: the stocks that RANKED HIGHEST in Dec 2025
were those that fell *least* during the crash, not those with the best momentum going in.

### Step 1D: Root Cause

**The LGBM model learned a value/mean-reversion signal, not momentum.**

Prediction correlations with features (on latest dataset snapshot):

| Feature | rho with prediction |
|---|---|
| `earnings_yield` | **+0.353** |
| `sharpe_ratio` | +0.261 |
| `quality_score` | +0.273 |
| `book_to_market` | +0.205 |
| `momentum_12_1` | +0.129 (weak) |
| `rsi` | **−0.213** |
| `price_to_sma200` | **−0.121** |
| `volatility_20d` | +0.065 |

**Model behavior:** "Give me stocks with HIGH earnings yield, OVERSOLD RSI,
and BELOW their 200-day moving average — they will mean-revert."

**Why it learned this:**

Dec 2025 snapshot dates have 76,819 resolved 63d labels landing Feb–Mar 2026.
During the tariff crash:
- High-momentum stocks (expensive, above SMA, high RSI) had the most to fall → ranked LOW
- Beaten-down cheap stocks (high earnings yield, below SMA, oversold) fell less → ranked HIGH

The model correctly learned what happened in that specific regime. The pattern is
real — value stocks DO outperform during market drawdowns. But it may be:
1. **Over-indexed** on crash-regime behavior (only ~13 weeks of crash data vs 9 years total)
2. **Generalizing poorly** to non-crash environments (momentum typically outperforms value)

**This is not a bug — it's a regime mismatch.** The model is predicting what would
happen if the next 63 days looked like Feb–Mar 2026. Whether that's correct depends
on the forward outlook.

## Part 2 — Calibration Fix (Implemented)

### Problem
Absolute return target `y = (return > 0)` collapsed to 95% SELL/STRONG SELL in
the current environment because:
1. Recent realized returns (Feb–Mar 2026) were negative for ~65–80% of stocks
2. VIX (25.78) and market_return_21d (−1.7%) in the feature vector dominated
   the logistic regression, pushing all predictions to near-zero probability

### Fix Applied

Three changes committed in `70470eb`:

**1. Relative return target**
```python
# Before
y = (resolved[ret_col] > 0).astype(int)

# After
if mkt_col in resolved.columns and resolved[mkt_col].notna().mean() > 0.5:
    y = (resolved[ret_col] > resolved[mkt_col]).astype(int)
else:
    y = (resolved[ret_col] > 0).astype(int)
```

**2. Remove VIX and market_return from calibration features**
Context features (VIX, market_return_21d) removed from `_build_calib_features`.
Calibration now trains on: model scores × 5, has_model × 5, agreement_top2,
spread_top2, sector_code → 13 features total (was 15).

**Rationale:** Market regime is already encoded in the raw model scores (LGBM has
`vix_level` and `spy_return_63d` as top features). The calibration layer should
focus on *which scores predict relative outperformance*, not *what the market will do*.

**3. Fix CalibratedProb/Signal not written to CSV**
Python operator precedence bug: `["CalibratedProb", "CalibratedSignal"] + [...] if x else []`
evaluated the entire list as `[]` when `calibrated_probs` dict was empty.

**Known remaining issues:**

1. **21d is nearly useless** — only 19.3% outperformance rate in training. This means
   during Feb–Mar 2026, SPY declined less than most individual stocks on a 21d horizon.
   The logistic regression with balanced class_weight struggles with 5:1 imbalance.
   Will self-correct as more live data accumulates.

2. **63d training window is narrow** — only Dec 2025 resolved rows (scores made Dec 8–24
   land fully in the 63d window). This is the same crash-regime data that biased the LGBM.
   The calibration for 63d is effectively "which model scores predicted outperformance
   during the tariff crash" — which may or may not generalize.

3. **SELL-heavy distribution** — even with relative targets, 70% of stocks are SELL/STRONG SELL.
   This reflects genuinely poor recent 63d IC on the backfill data. As live predictions
   mature (first 63d read ~June 2026), the model will recalibrate.

## Implications for Retraining

### Immediate (before next training run)

**Option A: Retrain on `forward_63d_market_excess_rank`**
The dataset already has `forward_63d_market_excess_rank` = (stock return − SPY return),
cross-sectionally ranked. This is the correct target for a screener — it directly
answers "which stocks beat the market?" and is immune to the absolute-return distortion
from the crash period.

Tradeoff: changes what the model is optimizing for (not total return, but alpha). Requires
evaluating whether WFCV IC changes materially.

**Option B: Retrain `lgbm_breakout_v10` with embargo on Dec 2025 labels**
Mask the Dec 2025 rows from the 63d target during training (or set their sample weight
to 0). This removes the crash-label contamination while keeping everything else.

Tradeoff: loses ~77k training rows from a recent, informative regime. The model may
generalize better to non-crash environments but will be less robust in future crashes.

**Option C: Add a separate crash-regime model**
Train a dedicated model on the tariff-crash period (Oct 2025–present) and blend it
at a low weight. Let the primary model stay momentum-focused and the secondary model
catch the mean-reversion signal.

Tradeoff: ensemble complexity increases. Hard to know when to activate the crash model.

### Medium-term (within next 2–4 weeks)

**LSTM fold 6 investigation (Parts 3 & 4 from the diagnostics doc):**
- 3A: Reversal-day IC decomposition (free, ~1 hour) — does LSTM fail specifically on big-move days?
- 3B: Feature distribution shift analysis (free, ~1 hour) — how far OOD are the 2024–2026 features?
- 4: Checkpoint weight drift — did the walk-forward fine-tuning actually adapt, or freeze?

These should run before committing to any LSTM retraining. Results may show that the
LSTM fold 6 collapse shares the same root cause as the LGBM value-bias (crash-era labels)
rather than being an architectural issue.

### Long-term

**Rebuild dataset with `--snapshot-freq daily`** when the infrastructure exists.
The LSTM in particular was noted to need daily snapshots for proper 1d/5d sequence
training. Currently 2,470 unique dates over 10 years ≈ weekly frequency.

**FCF yield, accruals, PEAD features** (deferred in feature backlog) — these would
complement the earnings_yield signal the model already uses and might improve the
value signal's quality vs. distress discrimination.
